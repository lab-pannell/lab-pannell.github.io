ECOLOGY & EVOLUTION
OF PLANT SEXUAL SYSTEMS

Welcome to the website of John Pannell's lab! Our group works on plant evolution in the Department of Ecology and Evolution of the University of Lausanne (Switzerland)

About Us

"There are several groups of plants in which all the species are di≈ìcious, and these exhibit no rudiments in the one sex of the organs proper to the other. About the origin of such plants nothing is known."

This quote by Charles Darwin from his book "The Different Forms Of The Same Species" (1877) is surprisingly still relevant today. Why dioecy in plants is rare and why species that are dioecious are so are questions that have not yet been fully resolved.

In the wake of these old questions, the group is particularly focused on addressing questions concerning transitions between sexual systems, and their implications for mating, resource allocation, demography and the evolution of the genome and transcriptome, especially sex chromosomes. Some of these are summarised under the project descriptions below. These projects use a combination of theory, wet-lab work, bioinformatics, and fieldwork. See our recent publications for further details of results from these and other projects.

UNVEILING PLANT INTRICACIES

Botanical research is a mesmerizing field that delves into the complexities of the plant world. From the tiniest moss to the tallest trees, plants exhibit an incredible diversity in their forms and functions.

Our group is focused on understanding the diverse and complex reproductive strategies of plants and how they are affected by environmental and genetic factors. We use a variety of experimental and computational approaches. Our experimental methods include field observations, laboratory experiments, and molecular and biochemical analyses to gather data on the genetics of sexual systems in a range of plant species. We also use computational techniques, such as statistical modeling and data analysis, to analyze and interpret this data and to test hypotheses about the factors that influence sex determination and sex allocation in plants. By combining experimental and computational approaches, we aim to gain a deeper understanding of the underlying mechanisms and evolutionary dynamics of sex determination and sex allocation in plants, and how these are shaped by a variety of evolutionary and ecological factors.
